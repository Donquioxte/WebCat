The person behind the tests

About me

Cybersecurity student, CTF player, and aspiring penetration tester based in Nairobi, Kenya.

Who I am

I'm Wil, a cybersecurity student based in Nairobi, Kenya, working toward a career as a professional penetration tester. I've been interested in how systems break since I first started tinkering with computers — the curiosity that gets most people who end up in this field.

Right now I'm building the skills and experience needed to work as a freelance pen tester, combining formal study with hands-on practice through CTF competitions, HackTheBox labs, and a growing number of real client engagements. This site is part portfolio, part learning log — a place to document what I'm building and what I'm figuring out along the way.

How I got here

My interest in security started the way it does for a lot of people — curiosity about how things work under the surface, and a tendency to ask "what happens if I do this instead?" rather than just following the intended path. That mindset translates well to offensive security, where the job is fundamentally about finding the gap between how a system was designed to behave and how it actually behaves when someone pushes on it.

I started with the basics — networking fundamentals, Linux, Python scripting — before moving into dedicated security study. PortSwigger's Web Security Academy was where most of my web application knowledge was built, and HackTheBox has been the main venue for putting it into practice. The write-ups on this blog are a direct record of that process: things I figured out, techniques that clicked, and the occasional rabbit hole that took longer than it should have.

What I'm working on

Currently studying for the OSCP certification, which requires demonstrating practical exploitation skills across a range of real machines under exam conditions. It's one of the most respected certifications in offensive security precisely because it can't be passed by memorising theory — you have to actually find and exploit vulnerabilities in the time available.

Alongside that, I'm building my freelance practice — taking on web application and network assessments for small businesses and startups, and using every engagement as an opportunity to tighten up my methodology and reporting. The goal is to be doing this full-time, and every engagement gets me closer to that.

Outside of security

When I'm not in a terminal or reading a CVE write-up, I'm usually exploring what Nairobi's tech scene has to offer — it's a genuinely exciting city to be working in security right now, with a fast-growing startup ecosystem and an increasing awareness of cybersecurity as a real business concern rather than an afterthought.

I'm also interested in the broader question of cybersecurity capacity in East Africa — the threat landscape here is real and growing, but the pool of local technical talent working in offensive security is still small. Building that capacity, even in a small way, feels like work worth doing.

Working with me

I work with clients in Nairobi and remotely across Kenya and internationally. Every engagement starts with a scoping call, signed paperwork, and clearly defined rules of engagement before any testing begins. I aim to be straightforward about what I can and can't do, and transparent about findings rather than softening reports to make them easier to read.

If you're a small business or startup thinking about security testing for the first time, I'm comfortable walking through what that process looks like and helping scope something that fits your budget and risk profile — not just sending over a price list.
